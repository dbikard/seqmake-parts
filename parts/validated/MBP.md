# MBP

MBP is the 396-aa Escherichia coli K12 maltose/maltodextrin-binding protein (MalE), the canonical high-efficiency solubility-enhancing and amylose-affinity fusion partner for recombinant protein production.

## Origin
MBP is the product of the *E. coli* K12 *malE* gene, a periplasmic component of the maltose/maltodextrin transport system. The gene and its product were determined by Duplay et al. (PMID 6088507), establishing a 396-aa precursor consisting of a 26-residue N-terminal Sec signal peptide (cleaved between Ala26 and Lys27 on export) and a 370-aa mature periplasmic protein (residues 27-396). The sequence stored here is the full precursor and matches the Swiss-Prot reviewed canonical entry UniProt P0AEX9 at 100% identity over its full length.

## Properties
The native protein binds maltose and linear maltodextrins; this ligand-binding activity underlies its use as an affinity handle, since maltose-bound MBP is captured on amylose resin and eluted with free maltose. As a fusion partner, MBP is uncommonly effective at promoting the solubility of the polypeptides to which it is fused, outperforming several other common partners (PMID 10452611). This solubility enhancement is an intrinsic property of the MBP fold rather than a generic chaperone effect; folding of the fused passenger is either spontaneous or dependent on endogenous chaperones (PMID 23166722). MBP fusions typically accumulate to a high level in *E. coli*, on the order of a few percent of total cellular protein. A caution for structural work: in a common engineered MBP variant, passenger sequences can promote interlaced (domain-swapped) dimers, which can confound oligomeric-state interpretation (PMID 31892719).

## Use
MBP is deployed as an N-terminal fusion partner to (i) enhance soluble expression of difficult passenger proteins and (ii) provide a one-step amylose-affinity purification handle (maltose elution) (PMID 2843437). A site-specific protease recognition sequence (e.g. for Factor Xa) is typically engineered into the linker so the MBP tag can be removed from the passenger after purification; the cleavage site lies in the linker, not within MBP itself (PMID 3073105). For cytoplasmic tagging the signal-less mature form (residues 27-396) is used, whereas the signal-peptide-bearing precursor directs periplasmic export. MBP is also frequently combined with an N-terminal His6 tag (His6-MBP) so the same fusion can be purified by either metal-affinity or amylose chromatography (PMID 21125392, 15922084).
