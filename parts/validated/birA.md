# birA

birA — E. coli bifunctional biotin-protein ligase / biotin-operon repressor (BirA, 321 aa); the parent enzyme of the AviTag biotinylation system and of the BioID/TurboID proximity-labeling ligases.

## Origin
birA encodes BirA, a 321-aa (~35.3 kDa) protein of *Escherichia coli* K12 (UniProt P06709). The gene was sequenced and its product characterized as a bifunctional biotin holoenzyme synthetase that also represses the biotin biosynthesis operon ([PMID 7024555](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/7024555/), [PMID 6456358](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/6456358/), [PMID 3899863](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/3899863/)). The crystal structure revealed an asymmetric three-domain fold: an N-terminal alpha-helical helix-turn-helix DNA-binding domain, a central seven-stranded mixed beta-sheet catalytic domain carrying the active site, and a C-terminal antiparallel beta-sheet domain ([PMID 1409631](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/1409631/), doi:[10.1073/pnas.89.19.9257](https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.89.19.9257)).

## Properties
BirA biotinylates substrates in two steps: it condenses biotin with ATP to form biotinoyl-5'-AMP, which is held in the active site, and then transfers biotin to a specific lysine of the acceptor protein ([PMID 22412018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22412018/), doi:[10.1083/jcb.201112098](https://doi.org/10.1083/jcb.201112098)). Bio-5'-AMP is also the corepressor: it drives BirA homodimerization, a prerequisite for cooperative binding of two holo-BirA monomers to the ~40-bp bioO operator palindrome, repressing the biotin operon ([PMID 10529178](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10529178/), [PMID 9485476](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9485476/), doi:[10.1021/bi9715019](https://doi.org/10.1021/bi9715019)). The biotin-binding region includes a GRGRRG loop (present here near residues 115-120, 1-based). The C-terminal domain is essential for catalysis ([PMID 11714929](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11714929/), doi:[10.1110/ps.22401](https://doi.org/10.1110/ps.22401)), and the wing of the N-terminal wHTH domain organizes the active site and shields the reactive intermediate ([PMID 24189073](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24189073/), doi:[10.1074/jbc.M113.525618](https://doi.org/10.1074/jbc.M113.525618)).

## Use
The enzyme's extreme substrate specificity underpins the AviTag/biotin-acceptor-peptide system: co-expressed with a protein bearing a short acceptor peptide (15-aa GLNDIFEAQKIEWHE, from a 13-residue consensus), BirA mono-biotinylates a single lysine, enabling avidin/streptavidin capture, detection and immobilization ([PMID 7764094](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/7764094/), doi:[10.1038/nbt1093-1138](https://doi.org/10.1038/nbt1093-1138); [PMID 18323822](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18323822/), doi:[10.1038/nprot.2008.20](https://doi.org/10.1038/nprot.2008.20)). Because endogenous biotinylation in eukaryotes is limited to a few carboxylases, background is low and the system functions across mammalian cells, yeast, flies and worms. Engineered descendants extend the enzyme to proximity proteomics: the R118G mutant (BirA*) loses self-association and DNA binding and binds bio-5'-AMP ~100-fold more weakly, releasing reactive intermediate to promiscuously biotinylate neighbours within ~20-30 nm (BioID; [PMID 22412018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22412018/)). Directed evolution produced TurboID (35 kDa, 15 mutations vs wild type) and miniTurbo (28 kDa, N-terminal domain deleted plus 13 mutations), which enable ~10-minute biotin labeling in cells and whole organisms ([PMID 30125270](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30125270/), doi:[10.1038/nbt.4201](https://doi.org/10.1038/nbt.4201)). Superrepressor mutants with enhanced dimerization further illuminate allosteric control ([PMID 22210766](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22210766/), doi:[10.1128/jb.06549-11](https://doi.org/10.1128/jb.06549-11)).
